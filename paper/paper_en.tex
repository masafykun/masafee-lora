\section{Introduction}
Text-to-image diffusion models have become widely available, and open models such as Stable Diffusion can now be used by individuals. However, generating a specific original character with a consistent appearance is not feasible with a general-purpose model alone; additional training is required.

LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation) trains only low-rank difference matrices instead of re-training the entire model, allowing a specific concept to be added with modest compute and few training images.

The motivation of this work is practical. The author created an original red-panda character, ``Masafee,'' published as a LINE sticker set of 16 stickers. The starting point is the desire to generate new poses and scenes of this character without drawing them by hand. While paid cloud computing was initially considered, this study verifies whether the task can be completed using only a single consumer-grade GPU. The specific questions are:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2.4em,topsep=2pt,itemsep=1pt]
  \item[\textbf{Q1.}] Can a character's identity be preserved while generating novel poses, using only $\sim$13 images?
  \item[\textbf{Q2.}] What factors govern output quality (base model, learning rate, LoRA rank, epoch count)?
  \item[\textbf{Q3.}] Does increasing the number of training epochs continue to improve quality?
\end{itemize}

\section{Background}
Stable Diffusion is a latent diffusion model; this study targets the widely-used SD~1.5 family. LoRA trains only a low-rank difference $\Delta W = BA$ of a weight matrix $W$, drastically reducing the number of trainable parameters. Training used \texttt{sd-scripts} by kohya-ss, one of the de facto standard implementations for LoRA training.

\subsection{Training Data}
The training data originated from the author-created LINE sticker set ``Masafee'' (16 stickers). As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:data}, the character is based on a red panda with consistent features: goggles, a crown-patterned bandana, and a ringed tail. Of the 16 stickers, one severely low-resolution image and two rough-sketch-style images were excluded, and \textbf{13} were used. All selected images are 1000$\times$1000\,px RGB.

\subsection{Caption Design}
A unique trigger word \texttt{masafee} was fixed at the start of every caption. Attributes intended to be variable (pose, expression, background, presence of text) were written explicitly so they would be learned as controllable concepts. Invariant character features (goggles, bandana) were deliberately not captioned, so they bind to the trigger word. Text baked into the stickers was handled by noting ``with text'' in captions rather than cropping.

\subsection{Training Configuration}
The optimizer was 8-bit AdamW, mixed precision was bfloat16, and LoRA was trained including the text encoder. The attention mechanism used PyTorch's native SDPA, avoiding a dependency on additional libraries.

\section{Experiments}
\textbf{Experiment 1 (Feasibility):} Initial training used standard SD~1.5 (\texttt{v1-5-pruned-emaonly}), 512\,px resolution, LoRA rank 32 / alpha 16, learning rate 1e-4, for 16 epochs (1040 steps).

\textbf{Experiment 2 (Factor comparison):} Six conditions varying base model \{SD1.5, Counterfeit-V3.0\}, learning rate \{1e-4, 2e-4\}, and LoRA rank \{16, 32\} were trained and compared at 768\,px, 30 epochs each. Each checkpoint was evaluated with five prompts absent from the training data.

\textbf{Experiment 3 (Epoch ablation):} The best condition from Experiment~2 was extended to 60 epochs, comparing outputs at epochs 30/40/50/60 with a fixed random seed.

\section{Results}
\subsection{Experiment 1: Initial Training Succeeds}
Initial training took about 9 minutes on the RTX 3060, using about 3\,GB of VRAM. The character's identity was reproduced well, and poses absent from the training data could be generated (\textbf{Q1 confirmed}). Two weaknesses appeared: (i) backgrounds turned dark even when ``white background'' was specified, and (ii) outputs had stronger shading than the flat original art style.

\subsection{Experiment 2: The Base Model Matters Most}
The six-condition comparison gave a clear result (Figure~\ref{fig:sweep}). The three standard-SD1.5-base conditions all darkened backgrounds and applied strong shading, while the three Counterfeit-base conditions produced clean white backgrounds and flat coloring close to the original art style. The weaknesses of Experiment~1 were resolved \textbf{not by tuning the learning rate or LoRA rank, but by switching the base model to an illustration-oriented one}. \textbf{For Q2, we conclude that the base model choice is dominant among the factors examined.} The best condition was Counterfeit + learning rate 1e-4 + LoRA rank 32 (cf\_lr1\_d32).

\subsection{Experiment 3: Epochs Plateau at About 30}
Extending the best condition to 60 epochs (Figure~\ref{fig:epoch}), the outputs at epochs 30/40/50/60 were nearly indistinguishable; \textbf{performance had already plateaued by epoch 30}. At epochs 50--60, saturation increased, showing slight overfitting. \textbf{For Q3, we conclude that increasing the epoch count beyond about 30 does not improve quality.}

\subsection{Generation with the Final Model}
Using the best model (cf\_lr1\_d32, epoch 30), 12 scenes absent from the training data were generated (Figure~\ref{fig:showcase}), all preserving the character's identity and flat art style at a quality sufficient for practical use. A practical lesson was that scene-dependent attributes such as background brightness should be specified explicitly in the prompt.

\section{Discussion}
\textbf{Dominance of the base model.} Output quality strongly depends on the base model. Choosing a base model that matches the target art style takes priority over hyperparameter tuning.

\textbf{Overfitting and epoch count.} With as few as 13 images, training saturates at about 30 epochs. The intuition that ``longer training is better'' is incorrect.

\textbf{Data size sets the quality ceiling.} Since the epoch count plateaus, the main remaining lever for further quality gains is adding training images.

\textbf{Sufficiency of consumer-grade GPUs.} Each run took 9--40 minutes on the RTX 3060, using 3--8\,GB of VRAM; paid cloud computing is not required for an SD1.5 character LoRA.

\section{Limitations and Future Work}
Evaluation was by visual inspection without quantitative metrics. Training used 13 images of a single character, limiting generalizability. Baked-in text was handled by captioning rather than cropping. Future work includes adding training images, introducing quantitative metrics, and validation with newer base models such as SDXL.

\section{Conclusion}
Using only a consumer-grade GPU (RTX 3060), we performed LoRA fine-tuning of the author's original character ``Masafee.'' We confirmed novel-pose generation with preserved identity from 13 images (Q1), that output quality depends most strongly on the base model (Q2), and that performance plateaus at about 30 epochs (Q3). Fine-tuning a personally-owned character can be completed practically on a single consumer-grade GPU, without paid cloud resources.
